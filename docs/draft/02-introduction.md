\subsection{Background and Conceptual Gap}

Higher education in the digital era faces a fundamental challenge in evaluating the EdTech platforms adopted for *high-stakes* language test preparation, specifically IELTS and TOEFL. The literature indicates that existing *Computer-Assisted Language Learning* (CALL) platform evaluation is fragmented: it either centres on linguistic aspects without considering information systems architecture, or conversely skews too technical without interrogating pedagogical validity~[b1][b2]. This disciplinary divide exists because Applied Linguistics researchers often lack the software engineering expertise to evaluate database structures, response latency, or scalability, while Information Systems (IS) researchers typically design generic technology adoption models that fail to capture domain-specific features like SLA (Second Language Acquisition) constraints. Consequently, universities frequently adopt technically robust platforms that fail pedagogically, or vice-versa. This gap is further compounded by the absence of any evaluation framework explicitly designed for the *test preparation* context, which demands precision metrics for *micro-learning*, *spaced repetition*, and alignment with international test standards~[b3].

A survey of the *International Journal of Educational Technology in Higher Education* (ETHE) and other leading *educational technology* journals reveals that conceptual research developing *theoretical frameworks* and *assessment models* for self-directed learning technologies receives significant attention, particularly when it bridges the disciplines of Information Systems and Applied Linguistics~[b4][b5]. Yet to date, no *theoretical framework* explicitly integrates the technology, pedagogy, and institutional dimensions into a single evaluation model for language test preparation platforms.

\subsection{Conceptual Research Questions}

Based on the gap identification above, three conceptual research questions are examined:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item How can a *theoretical framework* be constructed that synthesises technology acceptance theory, computer-assisted language learning theory, and *learning analytics* theory for evaluating high-stakes language test preparation platforms?
  \item How can an *assessment model* be formulated, complete with indicators and operational definitions, to evaluate evaluating platforms from a multi-stakeholder perspective (learners, instructors, administrators) at the conceptual level?
  \item How can the theoretical relationships among evaluation dimensions be mapped in a conceptual structural model that is amenable to empirical testing in the future?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Research Objectives}

Table~tab:objectives details the conceptual research objectives and corresponding outputs of this study. The formulation of these objectives is driven by the need to resolve the fragmented landscape of educational technology evaluation. Objective 1 focuses on synthesizing multidisciplinary concepts because a singular disciplinary approach is insufficient to capture both technical and linguistic success criteria. Objective 2 translates this synthesis into a multidimensional theoretical framework, which acts as the conceptual foundation. To move from abstract theory to practical utility, Objective 3 operationalizes the framework into specific indicators and rubrics, providing university administrators with an actionable decision-support tool. Finally, Objective 4 constructs a conceptual structural model to map the causal pathways of platform success, providing educational technology researchers with testable hypotheses for future empirical field studies.
